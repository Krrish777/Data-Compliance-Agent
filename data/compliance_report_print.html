Three rules carry 94.4% of your exposure. Here is what you need to know before the next board cycle.

This memo accompanies the first run of the Data Compliance Agent against our production transactions warehouse. The numbers are material. The fixes are tractable.

Over the weekend we ran the new compliance scanner against the transactions table — 5.1M rows, a single sitting. It returned 11,775 exceptions across 17 regulatory rules extracted from our RAFT policy document, producing a composite compliance score of 58.8% (Grade D). Seven rules are in a failing state; none sit in the review queue.

The distribution matters more than the total. Three rules alone — two on the paid leg, one on the received leg of large cash movements — account for 11,133 of those 11,775 exceptions. None of the three are failing because of data quality. They are failing because the flag was never wired into our reporting pipeline.

This is an engineering gap pretending to be a compliance gap. It is the cheapest class of finding we will ever receive.

The remaining findings are smaller in volume but significant in kind. Six Bitcoin transactions escaped enhanced due diligence. 554 million-dollar-plus transfers were not escalated. One record flagged for laundering in 2022 has never been reviewed — that last is a control-level failure, not a count, and regulators treat it as such.

I recommend we treat this as a 90-day engineering program with compliance oversight, not a compliance project with engineering support. The annotated roadmap at page 9 commits us to a first passing scan by 20 May 2026.

Case № LDR-001 · Row 4,743

On 01 September 2022, a transaction marked for laundering entered our ledger. It was never reviewed.

Four years later, it surfaced in this scan. Not as a data-quality issue, not as a false positive — but as exactly what the field engineers meant when they set is_laundering = 1: a transaction the system, at the time, believed was laundering.

Policy §5.1 requires all flagged transactions to be reviewed by the compliance team within 48 hours. The scanner found no evidence of review on this record — no status change, no reviewer ID, no timestamp. The 48 hours elapsed in September 2022; what we are looking at now is 1,279 days of silence on a record the system asked us to look at.

The count on this rule is one. The control it fails is not.

Part V · Two findings in depth

FMT-003 and LDR-001 — filed in full.

I
FMT-003
3,711Violations filed
High · Fail

Transactions using Cash with Amount Paid exceeding 10,000 shall be flagged.

Explanation

Every qualifying row in the scan window breaches the threshold. The Amount Paid column is populated correctly; the flagging mechanism is simply absent from the SAR/CTR path. This is a structural gap, not a data-quality one.

Policy

§3.2 — Large Transaction Reporting. Applies when payment_format = 'Cash' AND amount_paid > 10,000.

Business risk

Unfiled CTRs on cash transactions above the threshold are the single most cited AML failure in BSA enforcement actions against depositories.

Remediation · ordered steps
  1. Install a CTR flag hook on amount_paid > 10,000 AND payment_format = 'Cash'.
  2. Back-fill the 3,711 historical rows into the SAR/CTR queue.
  3. Run a verification scan and certify to the audit committee.
II
LDR-001
1Violation filed
High · Fail

Flagged transactions must be reviewed by the compliance team within forty-eight hours.

Explanation

Row 4,743 carries is_laundering = 1 and has never been reviewed. A single record — but the control itself has failed for 1,279 days. Regulators audit the control, not the record.

Policy

§5.1 — Flagged Transaction Review. Applies whenever is_laundering = 1 and review_status is null or stale > 48h.

Business risk

A lapsed review on a laundering-flagged record invites a control-level finding. Remediating it after discovery does not reset the clock on the breach itself.

Remediation · ordered steps
  1. Review row 4,743 and record the outcome.
  2. Instrument the 48-hour SLA with an alert for future breaches.
  3. Back-scan historical laundering-flagged records for similar lapses.
